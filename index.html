Inspiration

I wanted to pick a topic that interested me and I chose this dataset of Austin animal shelter because I felt it can be used to analyze adopters and pets they adopted. I'm an adopter myself. My two lovely cats were originally stray cats. They were first registered at a regional shelter and were later transferred to a nearby shelter before they finally came to my home.

In most cases, adopters choose to sign up on a waiting list at local shelter. Last year when I joined the waiting list, I clearly knew what I preferred to adopt: kittens that are young and healthy. Additionally I preferred domestic shorthair as this breed is known for being strong and energetic. I wonder how others think when they join the waiting list and what factors have affected their adoption decision? I hope this dataset will give me some insights.

Overview of the dataset

Austin Animal Center Shelter

The largest U.S. animal shelter dataset

The Austin Animal Center is the largest no-kill animal shelter in the United States that provides care and shelter to over 18,000 animals each year. As part of the AAC's efforts to help and care for animals in need, the organization makes available its accumulated data and statistics as part of the city of Austin's Open Data Initiative.

Content of Dataset: Intakes & Outcomes 2013 ~ 2018

The data contains intakes and outcomes of animals entering the Austin Animal Center from the beginning of October 2013. The datasets are also freely available on the Socrata Open Data Access API and are updated daily. For this visualization project, I selected a Kaggle dataset which is a merged version of raw intakes and outcomes data from October 2013 to March 2018.

For more information about the dataset, please refer to:

Socrata's Open Data:
- Austin Animal Center Intakes
- Austin Animal Center Outcomes

Kaggle:
- Austin Animal Center Shelter Intakes and Outcomes

Overview of the dataset

Austin Animal Center Intakes

Count by Intake Type

Animal Intakes (2013 ~ 2018)

Intakes represent the status of animals as they arrive at the Animal Center. The largest source of shelter animals is stray, taking about 80% of the total numbers. Other intake types include Owner Surrender, Public Assist, Wildlife and Euthanasia Request. Only 251 cases out of 79,672 records are Euthanasia Request (0.3%), which agrees with its reputation of being the largest "No Kill" shelter in the country.

Overview of the dataset

Austin Animal Center Outcomes

Count by Outcome Type

Animal Outcomes (2013 ~ 2018)

Outcomes represent the status of animals as they leave the Animal Center. Annually over 90% of animals entering the center are adopted, transferred to rescue or returned to their owners. Only 6244 outcomes out of 79,672 records are Euthanasia (7.8 %), which qualifies the shelter as "No Kill" shelter by maintaining the live save rate higher than 90%.

What factors will affect adoption?



I’m interested to know what are the major considerations when people decide to adopt pets from a shelter. Based on my own judgment, I have classified three outcome types as positive outcomes and the rest as negative outcomes. In other words, I want to figure out what parameters contribute more towards the positive outcomes and what parameters contribute more towards the negative outcomes.



Positive Outcome: Adoption, Return to Owner and Rto-Adopt

Negative Outcome: Transfer, Euthanasia, Died, Disposal, Missing and Relocate



After screening all the fields of dataset, I have identified two primary factors and three secondary factors.



Primary factors: contain a common trend that is shared among all animal types.

Secondary factors: contain a trend that is relevant to a specific animal type.

Why adopting pets?

Did you know that over 1,000 people per hour run a search right here looking to adopt a pet? Pet adoption is quickly becoming the preferred way to find a new dog or cat, and rightly so, there are many benefits to adopting a pet. Pet adoption fees are usually much lower than buying from a breeder. You’re also likely to find a pet who’s already learned a few things. Adoptable pets are often already housetrained, good with kids, and do well with other pets.

By signing up on a waiting list, you will have the chance to adopt pets that perfectly satisfy your needs (e.g. sex, age, health, month, breed, etc.)
And don’t forget the wonderful feeling you get from saving a life!

Health

Two-class Outcome Percentage by Health (Dog)

More people are willing to adopt cats in non-normal conditions than dogs in non-normal conditions. The percentage of normal dogs ended up having positive outcome versus negative outcome is 77% to 23%. However this difference is not significant for normal cats, which is 53% to 47%. The reason behind it might relate to an intrinsic difference between cats and dogs -- dogs are pure outdoor animals but majority of cats are kept indoor. Consequently, physical diasability or compromised mobility will affect cats' popularity less than dogs' popularity.

Month

Two-class Outcome Percentage by Month (Dog)

Cats adoption has a seasonal popularity shift while dogs adoption is almost consistent throughout the year. The first peak of cats adoption is in July and the second peak is in December, which happen to align with the twice-a-year birthing season of cats. This phenomenon infers that cat adopters appear stronger desire than dog adopters to welcome pets in their early stage, possibly due to the fact that cats are considered "egoists" and are less clingy to their owner when they become older.

Breed

Two-class Outcome Percentage by Month (Dog)

Few cat breeds have distinguished popularity among cats adopters while dogs adopters focus less on breed. Positive outcome of the top two popular cat breeds exceeds the average cat positive outcome by 24.5% and 19.6%. However positive outcome of the top two popular dog breeds exceeds the average dog positive outcome by merely 4.7% and 2.9%. What does that mean? Firstly, overall positive outcome percentage of cats is much fewer than that of dogs (53% vs 76%), therefore smaller denominator leads to larger variance. Secondly, generally speaking cat adopters always have a larger set of options. So, sheltered cats need something special to stand out, for example, rare breeds.
